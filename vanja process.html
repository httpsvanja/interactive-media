Week 2 - Deep Dive into Infinite Numbers

I decided to look more into why this pattern, that is seen in rivers and branches, exists. How does it diverge and why can we see it in so many places?

A quick google search showed me that this is something called fractals, a term which i was already familiarised with a little bit. A fractal is a shape that infinitely repeats itself ‚Äî and a great example of this is broccoli ‚Äî when you cut a broccoli in half you‚Äôll see that at the top there are many smaller branches which when zoomed into resemble a whole broccoli.

This is actually quite a simple formula ‚Äî wherever there is one line it will always diverge into two (or sometimes more) and that line will also do the same, until, I guess, the pattern decides it‚Äôs had enough.

I also recognised the shape at the top of this illustration ‚Äî the Sierpinski Triangle, which is a triangle that gains a new triangle inside of it with each iteration, eventually growing into an infinitely zoomable triangle. Even though the output isn‚Äôt the same, I‚Äôve never before really considered this to be the exact same algorithm present in tree branches.

Another example of this is the Koch snowflake, a cousin of the Sierpinski Triangle. Similarly, it is also a triangle which gets another triangle added to each of it‚Äôs sides with each iteration, eventually turning into an infinitely zoomable snowflake.

Wanting to learn more about these fractals and how their algorithms actually work, I decided to look into infinite numbers, since for example the Sierpinski Triangle is the sum of all infinite numbers. This included a lot of math that I simply did not understand, well I do understand it in theory, but the more complicated the formulas I was reading were, the less I understood.

Week 3 - More Deep Diving

How do these fractals apply to the tree branches I was examining in Sonsbeek? Are they completely the same thing or are there differences?

Well, the main difference between the fractals like the Koch snowflake and branches on trees is infinity. Theoretically a branch could keep growing forever, but in reality it never does.

Another difference is that when a tree branch diverges into two smaller branches, it picks a dominant one, making the other one weaker and less likely to continue and sprout new branches. These smaller branches then also have a higher chance of falling off and disappearing, giving a wavy illusion to the branch.

(All of these statements are just observations I‚Äôm making and may not be completely accurate.)

All of these weird angles we see in this tiny branch are places where it has previously diverged into two, but the smaller, less dominant branch fell off. Often times the more dominant branch is the straighter one, but it doesn‚Äôt necessarily have to be the case.

This is a great example of a less straight branch being dominant, probably because the straighter one was broken.

(This narrowing down of elements already helped me understand the algorithm behind tree branches better, and luckily for me it doesn‚Äôt have much to do with infinite numbers.)

Sometimes new branches seem to be able to sprout of old places where the less dominant branches fell of, these are notably tinier and seem to diverge at marginally larger angles than other branches.

I decided to go to google and check if my theories of branches and dominance had some truth to it, and to my surprise it was actually correct.
